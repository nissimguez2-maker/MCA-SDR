# Pass 2 Audit: Deal Strategist

**Lane:** Positioning and win strategy for the re-up play (opportunity 1) and UCC-sourced near-payoff merchants (opportunity 4); deal control, competitive differentiation, and factor-rate shopping prevention across all qualified conversations.

---

**Strategy:** The solo broker's only durable edge against Lendio, Fundera/NerdWallet, and the 8-12 ISOs hitting the same UCC-sourced merchant is pre-call intelligence combined with a single compelling question that shifts the merchant's evaluation criteria before the shopping conversation starts. Aggregators win on breadth and brand; direct funders win on rate. The solo broker wins on specificity: "I already know your situation before we talk" is not a warm-fuzzy, it is a disqualifying landmine against competitors who arrive cold. For the re-up play, the compelling event is the merchant's own payoff date, not a sales pitch. For the UCC cold play, the compelling event is the near-payoff window: the merchant is about to be free of the factor-rate drag and most likely already being re-called by their original funder. Arrive first with the right framing and own the re-finance narrative before the original funder does.

---

**Tactics**

1. **Re-up play: call within the clawback window blindspot, not after.** Calculate the approximate payoff date from the funded date and daily debit amounts you can back-calculate from bank statements you already have. Target calls at 60-70% payoff, not 100%. At 100% the merchant is already hearing from the original funder. At 60-70%, the merchant is cash-strapped from the daily debits but not yet being re-solicited. The opener is not "would you like more funding" but "I see you took an advance around [date], you're probably about halfway through the payback right now; do you have a number in mind for what you need free and clear before you'd consider another round?" This is a pain question, not a pitch. If they confirm the payback math, the Economic Buyer (the owner, almost always) has just self-qualified. If they don't engage, they're not in the window yet. Log and re-queue at 85% payoff.

2. **UCC near-payoff cold play: lead with the filing date, not a product.** The opener that separates from the other 8-12 ISO callers is referencing the public record directly: "We pulled UCC filings in [state], I see [funder name] filed a lien against your business around [month/year]. I'm calling because merchants at that stage usually have a decision to make about whether to renew with the same funder or shop their options before the payoff. I don't know your situation yet, so I have two questions before I'd even recommend anything." This signals homework, establishes non-desperation, and immediately introduces optionality as the decision frame. The two questions: what their daily or weekly debit is, and whether they have any equipment or payroll needs coming up in the next 60 days. Equipment or payroll is the compelling event. If none, the compelling event is the cost of not shopping: most merchants do not know their original funder will re-offer at the same or higher factor rate as the default renewal.

3. **Kill factor-rate shopping by controlling the decision criteria, not by quoting low.** Factor-rate shopping wars are lost before they start; the solo broker cannot match the direct funder's cost of capital on rate and should not try. The landmine question to plant in discovery: "Before your last advance, did anyone walk you through the difference between two funders at 1.29 vs 1.35 on a 6-month term vs a 12-month term?" Most merchants do not understand that a lower factor rate on a shorter term has a higher effective daily cost. Walking them through the payback math for 90 seconds reframes the decision criteria from "who has the lowest factor" to "who is giving me the best daily cash flow outcome for my use of funds." Once the merchant is thinking in daily debit terms rather than factor rate, the solo broker who did that education owns the frame. Do not share this math with a merchant who is clearly shopping for the cheapest quote; they will use it to negotiate against you with the direct funder.

4. **MEDDPICC applied in two minutes per MCA call.** MCA sales cycles are 1-5 days, not weeks, so the framework compresses. The critical letters are: Identify Pain (what is the cash event in the next 30-60 days), Economic Buyer (is this the owner/signer, or is someone else signing), Decision Process (are they talking to other funders right now, and who else is involved), and Competition (did the original funder already call back). Skip Metrics and Paper Process at this stage; they live inside the document collection step. If the owner is not on the call, do not advance the deal. Period. A merchant who says "I have to talk to my partner/accountant/spouse" without that person joining the call in 24 hours is not a pipeline deal; it is a shopping delay. Re-queue and call back with both parties, or move the deal to Tier 3.

5. **Use the qualification page to pre-set the decision criteria before the call.** The page's payback calculator (opportunity 2) is not just a warming tool; it is a competitive landmine against cold ISOs. A merchant who has run the calculator and seen that a 1.30 factor rate at 6 months means $X daily debit has already been educated on the right criteria. When an ISO calls and leads with "I can get you 1.25," the merchant who has seen the calculator knows to ask about term length. Embed a line in the calculator output: "Factor rate means nothing without term length; ask any funder you speak with for the daily debit amount on the same term." This is compliant, honest, and shifts the evaluation criteria toward the solo broker who set the frame.

---

**What to avoid**

- Do not open re-up calls with a product offer or rate quote. The merchant will immediately shop the quote. Open with a pain question about the payback stage.
- Do not pursue a merchant who names three other funders they are "comparing." That is an active shopping conversation. The right response is: "Let me know when you're down to two; that's when the conversation gets useful." Log and re-contact in 48 hours. Chasing a three-funder shopping war is a commission lottery at 5%.
- Do not stack the landmine math approach with a low-rate pitch. Pick one frame. The "daily debit math" frame and the "cheapest rate" frame are contradictory; using both destroys credibility.
- Do not call without a lead brief. Arriving cold to a known re-up or UCC contact is a waste of the "I already know your situation" edge. Even two minutes of UCC filing review before the call creates the separation.

---

**Over-built or cut**

The plan's three-agent architecture adds latency to the deal-control loop. For opportunity 1 and opportunity 4, the most time-sensitive part of the win strategy is first-call quality and speed of follow-up, not architectural elegance. The Sales Intelligence Agent's lead brief is load-bearing. The Warm Lead Engine's scoring is load-bearing. The Operator/Secretary Agent's document chase is load-bearing. What is not load-bearing in this lane: any multi-step automated "nurture sequence" aimed at merchants who are in an active UCC near-payoff window. Those merchants should receive one outbound call and one voicemail or SMS, then be re-queued in 4 days if no response. An automated drip sequence on a UCC-sourced merchant is noise that trains them to ignore your number before the call that matters.

---

**Top risks**

1. **Original funder calls first and re-commits the merchant before you reach them.** The UCC window is perishable. A 60-150 day post-filing filter is correct but generic; the actual re-engagement call from the original funder often happens at day 90-110 when their own collections team starts outbound. If UCC processing takes more than one week from filing date to first call, the window compresses sharply. Build the UCC pull-to-first-call cycle time into week-one KPIs: target under 5 business days from filing date to call, and prioritize filings from 60-100 days ago over filings from 130-150 days ago.

2. **Compliant transparency backfires as a positioning claim if the call is not actually transparent.** "I'll be straight with you about costs" is a trust-builder only if the call that follows delivers it. If the merchant gets submitted to a funder at a factor the broker didn't disclose before submission, the transparency positioning is actively harmful to referral potential and repeat business, which are the only long-cycle assets this operator can build in 90 days. Compliance posture (plan section 11) must extend into verbal disclosures on every call, not just the page.

3. **Single-threaded deal death on no-signer calls.** In MCA, the Economic Buyer and the decision-maker are almost always the same person (the owner/signer), but not always: spouses, silent partners, and CFOs in small businesses can block a deal that looks won. The plan has no current protocol for multi-party deal situations. Add a qualifier to the Tier 1 lead brief: is the contact confirmed as the signer on the application, and if not, what is the step to get the signer on a call before submitting docs?

---

**Cross-lane flags**

- Opportunity 3 (document collection engine): the 48-hour chase discipline is a deal-control mechanism, not just an admin function. The post-call document request draft should include the specific use-of-funds framing from the call so the merchant's urgency is mirrored back in the ask. Flag to the Loan Officer / Operations lens.
- Opportunity 5 (call queue ranking by clawback survivability): the ranking model needs to encode not just merchant profile but deal-control signals from the call, specifically whether the Economic Buyer was confirmed as the signer, and whether a compelling event (cash need in 30-60 days) was named. A perfectly profiled merchant with no compelling event is not a Tier 1 deal. Flag to the Revenue Analyst / Prioritization lens.
- Qualification page (opportunity 2): the payback calculator doubles as a competitive positioning tool. The copy decisions on the calculator output are deal-strategy decisions, not just UX decisions. Flag to the Copywriter / Page lens.
- TCPA one-to-one consent for UCC-sourced contacts: these are cold outbound calls to merchants who have not opted in through the page. Whether a UCC filing constitutes a business contact reachable without prior express written consent under the Jan 2025 FCC rule is a compliance question that directly affects the viability of opportunity 4 as a calling strategy. Flag to the Compliance lens.

**Facts to verify**

- At what payoff percentage (60%, 70%, 80%) does the original MCA funder typically initiate their own re-up outbound? This determines the optimal call timing window for opportunity 1.
- Does the Jan 2025 FCC TCPA one-to-one consent rule apply to B2B calls to a business owner's cell phone when the lead source is a public UCC-1 filing? The answer affects whether opportunity 4 can be a calling strategy or is limited to direct mail and email.
- What factor rates and terms do the top 5 direct MCA funders currently offer for re-up renewals to existing borrowers vs. new ISO-submitted files? This quantifies whether the ISO commission structure leaves room to beat the direct funder's renewal offer on a net daily-debit basis.
- Is the "I already know your situation" opener (referencing the UCC filing by name) compliant under state commercial disclosure laws in TX, NY, FL, and CA, or does it require a specific disclosure that the information came from a public records search?
